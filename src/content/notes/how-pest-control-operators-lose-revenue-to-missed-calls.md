Pest control runs on inbound calls. Customers don't fill out web forms when they find mice under the stove. They call. Which means your ability to answer the phone — not just sometimes, but consistently, across peak hours and after hours and lunch and the dispatcher's bathroom break — is directly tied to your revenue.

Most operators know this in theory. Few have run the numbers.

## The call volume behind the problem

We analyzed 12 months of call records for a Metro Vancouver pest control operator — 1,346 calls, January through December 2025. The goal was to understand what was coming in, when it was coming in, and whether the existing intake process was handling it cleanly.

The classified call volume broke down predictably: mice and rats together accounted for 331 calls, or 39% of the calls where we could identify a primary pest. Bed bugs, ants, raccoons, and wasps made up most of the rest. Nothing surprising there.

What was interesting was the shape of the volume across months.

Rodent calls peaked in December (56 combined mice and rats) and November (44), with a secondary peak in January through February. Wasp calls peaked in July (15) and August (11). These peaks correspond directly to the times when service businesses are running reduced crews or stretched staffing: the holiday window, the summer surge.

This is the structural problem. Call volume is highest when coverage is thinnest. A dispatcher who handles 10 calls a day fine in March is fielding the same job in December while also managing holiday scheduling, reduced crew availability, and the general chaos of end-of-year operations. The calls that go to voicemail in December are not random — they're the calls that couldn't get through the bottleneck.

## What a missed call actually costs

Let's run the math that most operators don't run.

A typical residential rodent job — exclusion, treatment, follow-up — runs $350–600 depending on severity and property size. Call it $450 as a working average. A wasp nest removal runs $150–300. Bed bug treatment is higher, $500–1,200 for a residential unit.

If a caller goes to voicemail and doesn't get a callback within two hours, the research on callback conversion is consistent: conversion rates drop sharply. Some callers book with the next operator they reach. Some don't book at all because the urgency passes. A reasonable estimate for conversion on a same-hour answered call versus a next-day callback is 60–70% versus 20–30%, depending on the urgency of the job.

Apply that to a business seeing 840 qualifying calls a year — the classified volume from our dataset. If 15% of those calls go to voicemail or reach a dispatcher who can't give the call full attention, that's 126 calls. At the difference in conversion rates (call it a 40-point gap), you're losing roughly 50 jobs. At $450 average ticket, that's $22,500 in revenue that walked out the door not because you couldn't do the work, but because you couldn't answer the phone.

That number will be wrong for your specific business. The math is illustrative, not precise. But run it with your own numbers: your call volume, your ticket size, your voicemail rate, your callback conversion rate. The output usually surprises operators who haven't done it before.

## Why this happens to good operators

The missed-call problem isn't a staffing failure. It's a structural mismatch.

Human dispatchers are good at complex jobs: handling complaints, managing multi-site commercial accounts, diagnosing unusual situations. They are expensive to have on the phone for routine booking calls. And they don't scale — you can't ask your dispatcher to simultaneously handle four calls that come in at 7pm in November.

The operators who lose the most revenue to missed calls tend to share a few characteristics:

**They're handling inbound at the owner-operator level.** The owner answers the phone because no one else does. When the owner is in the field, on a ladder, in a crawlspace — the phone goes to voicemail.

**They have a single dispatcher who is also doing three other things.** Scheduling, customer callbacks, supplier orders, technician coordination. When a new call comes in during a busy window, it becomes one more thing on the list.

**Their after-hours coverage is voicemail.** Every call that comes in after 5pm goes to voicemail. For rodent calls — which often come from people who just found evidence of an infestation at home in the evening — that's the highest-urgency window, and it's completely unattended.

None of these are signs of a badly-run business. They're the natural result of growing past "owner answers everything" without having built the infrastructure to replace that coverage cleanly.

## Three ways operators fix it

There are three options, and they're worth evaluating in order of cost and trade-offs:

**Option 1: Hire a front-desk person or additional dispatcher.** Solves the coverage problem during business hours. Adds $1,500–$3,500/month in payroll depending on hours and market. Doesn't solve after-hours. Doesn't solve peak-surge windows when one dispatcher can't handle simultaneous calls. Creates a new single point of failure.

**Option 2: Answering service.** Costs $50–$200/month. Solves coverage broadly. The trade-off: an answering service takes a message. It doesn't qualify the lead, answer FAQs, or book the job. The caller gets "someone will call you back" — which lands you back in the callback-conversion problem. You've solved the answer rate. You haven't solved the conversion rate.

**Option 3: AI voice agent.** Costs more than an answering service, less than a dispatcher. Solves both coverage and conversion for qualifying calls — the routine booking calls that make up the majority of your inbound volume. Doesn't replace a dispatcher for complex situations; routes those to a human with full context.

The right choice depends on your call volume and ticket size. For businesses processing 500+ qualifying calls a year, the economics of option three tend to work clearly. Below that threshold, the math is closer.

## Where to start

If you've never measured your voicemail rate, start there. Pull the last 30 days of call records. Count how many went to voicemail versus were answered. If you can, track callback conversion separately — how many voicemail callers booked versus how many answered callers booked.

Most operators who run this analysis find the voicemail rate is higher than they expected. The conversion gap is almost always larger.

From there, the question is which of the three options fits your margin structure. The discovery call is the right place to work through the numbers for your specific business.
