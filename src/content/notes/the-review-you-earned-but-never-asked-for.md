A pest control tech wraps a wasp nest removal, the homeowner is genuinely relieved, says something like "you guys were fast, thank you." Three weeks later that customer is one row in a spreadsheet, and the five-star review they would have happily left never got left. Not because they were unhappy. Because nobody asked, and they forgot.

This is the most common gap I see in local service businesses, and it is the cheapest one to close.

Reviews on your Google Business Profile are not a vanity metric. For a plumber or an HVAC shop, review count and how recent those reviews are feed directly into whether you show up in the map pack when someone three blocks away searches "emergency furnace repair." Volume and recency. A business with 140 reviews and three from this month beats the one with 90 reviews where the last one landed last summer. You can do everything else right and still lose that placement because your review pipeline went quiet.

Here is the thing: the problem is almost never unhappy customers. Most service businesses I work with do good work. The problem is that asking is a manual step, and manual steps die first when the crew is slammed. The owner means to text the customer. The tech meant to mention it. Three jobs later it is gone.

That is exactly the kind of task an agentic workflow is built for. Not a chatbot. A workflow that watches for a trigger and acts on it without anyone having to remember.

The shape of it: the job gets marked complete in your CRM, or the invoice gets paid. That is the trigger. The workflow waits a sensible window, usually a few hours to a day, long enough that the customer has the result in hand but not so long they have moved on. Then it sends a text, because texts get read and review-request emails get buried. The text is short, it uses their name, and it drops a direct link straight to the review form so there is no hunting. If they do not act, one polite follow-up a couple of days later. That is it. No third nudge, no pestering.

Now the honest parts, because this is where people get it wrong.

You cannot filter who you ask. Google's policy prohibits "review gating," which means only soliciting reviews from customers you already know are happy. People build workflows that screen for a thumbs-up first and route only the happy ones to Google. That is against the rules, and Google has gotten better at catching it. What you can do, and should, is give every customer an easy path to reach you directly if something went sideways. A line like "anything we missed? reply here and I'll call you" sends a frustrated customer to you before they send themselves to a public one-star. That is not gating. That is service. The difference matters, and it is worth getting right.

Timing and tone are real tradeoffs. Send too soon and you look like you care more about the review than the work. Send too late and they have forgotten the job. Sound too automated and it reads as spam. The text has to sound like it could have come from the owner's phone, because functionally it did.

And you need clean data and consent. The workflow is only as good as the contact info in your system, and you need permission to text people. Garbage phone numbers, and you are texting strangers. Skip consent, and you have a different kind of problem.

The honest limit: this amplifies, it does not manufacture. If the work is mediocre, automating the ask just collects mediocre reviews faster. The workflow assumes you have earned the five stars and are simply failing to collect them. For most of the shops I talk to, that assumption holds. They are sitting on goodwill they never converted into anything Google can see.

The math is not complicated. A shop doing 80 jobs a month that turns even an extra 20 percent of them into reviews is adding a steady stream of fresh, recent reviews every single month, on autopilot, for roughly the cost of the texts. Compare that to what they spend on ads to rank for the exact searches that reviews help them win for free.

You already did the hard part. You showed up, you fixed the thing, the customer was happy. The review was earned weeks ago. All that is missing is the ask, and the ask is the one part you should never have to remember.
